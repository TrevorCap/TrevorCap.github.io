Linear Regression

In order to predict the strikeout and walk rates we can use simple linear regression. This is a simple, straightforward prediction method that is easy to understand and explain. This simplicity, however can be a drawback as it cannot understand relationships that are more complex. For instance, if there were diminshing returns above a certain point, a linear regression would not understand this difference. It also cannot be delimited, meaning extreme samples may produce improbable results (example: a strikeout rate of 120%). The results of the linear regression detailed on the Methodology page are found below. The stats used are for the 2019 season up to April 27, 2019.


Strikeout Rates

For expected strikeout rates we are able to use the linear regression for data in April 2019 and come back with basically no correlation and predictions that are wildly improbable. Even though logically, we know that no pitcher could have any rate stats except between 0 and 1, we end up with figures of nearly negative 200%! This prediction is functionally useless.

Walk Rates

For expected walk rates we do even worse, and as a matter of fact, our correlation is actually, if anything, negative! This means that our predictions are significantly worse than random.

Strikout Minus Walk Rates

Obviously, then, combining these two predictions does even worse. This line of best fit is even misleading, because visually we can see that these numbers are more or less randomly distributed among figures that would not make sense as predictions.
