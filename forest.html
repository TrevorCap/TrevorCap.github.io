Random Forest Regressor

In order to predict the strikeout and walk rates with machine learning, we can use random forest regression. This is a simple, straightforward way of implementing machine learning to attempt to predict pitcher outcomes. When regressing the figures with the Random Forest Regressor, we are able to predict the strikeouts, walks, and combined strikeout minus walk rates for pitchers with hopefully, more limited data. The drawback to this method is that it is more or less a black box. While a decision tree is simple enough to understand, the actual mechanics of this type of regression are not apprant, other than the weight assigned to each variable.

In order to put this regression to practical use, we use the current data up to April 27, 2019 in order to attempt to identify under and over-achieving pitchers.


Strikeout Rates

For expected strikeout rates we are able to use the data from April 2019 and come back with an R2 of 0.45. Considering that this data is from April, and as such, is a relatively small sample, this is a strong result. Being able to explain approximately half of the variation in a pitcher's strikeout rates can give us a general idea of who may be under or over performing relative to their underlying performance.

Walk Rates

For expected walk rates we see a much more significant decline in the prediction quality. Whereas in the in-sample testing, we were able to fit 89% of the variance in a given pitcher's walk rate, with the smaller sample of April, we come away with only 30% of the variation explained by our prediction. Better than nothing, but still needing improvement.

Strikout Minus Walk Rates

For expected strikeout minus walk rates we are able to use the data from April 2019 and come back with an R2 of 0.23. Considering that this ratio incorporates the two that we predicted separately, it is unsurpring to see this figure be less accurate than either of the other two.
